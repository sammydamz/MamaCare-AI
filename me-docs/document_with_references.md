1. # **SECTION A: PROBLEM STATEMENT**

   1. ## THE SCALE OF THE PROBLEM

Every year, women die from pregnancy complications that could have been prevented. According to the World Health Organization, approximately 287,000 women died from pregnancy-related causes in 2020 alone. That is nearly 800 women every single day. More than 94% of these deaths happened in low and middle-income countries, and sub-Saharan Africa accounts for about 70% of all maternal deaths worldwide.\[1\]

In West Africa, the maternal mortality ratio stands at approximately 542 deaths per 100,000 live births.\[2\] The gap is not explained by the complexity of the medical problems. The WHO estimates that more than 75% of all maternal deaths are preventable with timely care.\[3\] Women are not dying because we lack the knowledge to save them. They are dying because the care does not reach them in time.

2. ##  THE GAP BETWEEN APPOINTMENTS

Most maternal care is built around clinic visits. A woman attends her appointment, receives advice, and goes home. What happens between that visit and the next one is largely invisible to the health system.

This gap is where most deaths occur. Research shows that the majority of maternal deaths happen not during facility deliveries, but in the days and weeks before and after, when women are at home and out of contact with care providers. Data across multiple countries in sub-Saharan Africa shows that fewer than 43% of women received the recommended four or more antenatal care visits.\[4\] Postnatal care coverage is even lower, with fewer than 30% of women receiving a check within two days of delivery, which is the period of highest risk.\[5\]

Preeclampsia and eclampsia, which account for about 14% of all maternal deaths globally,\[6\] are among the most detectable complications when monitoring is continuous. Postpartum bleeding, responsible for roughly 27% of maternal deaths worldwide,\[7\] is treatable in most cases when it is caught early. These conditions are not killing women because they are untreatable. They are killing women because no one is watching closely enough between appointments.

3. ## EXISTING TOOLS ARE NOT REACHING THE RIGHT PEOPLE

Digital health tools have grown rapidly over the last decade. Mobile applications, SMS reminders, and telemedicine services have all been introduced with the aim of improving maternal care. Many have shown results in the right settings. But almost all of them assume that the woman using them has a smartphone, a data connection, and the ability to navigate a digital interface.

That assumption fails in the communities where maternal mortality is highest. Across sub-Saharan Africa, fewer than one in three people in rural communities have access to mobile internet.\[8\] Women are also less likely than men to own a phone or use mobile internet, with the gap running as high as 20% in low and middle-income countries.\[9\]

Basic voice calls tell a different story. Mobile network coverage reaches over 90% of the African population, including rural and low-income communities.\[10\] A solution built on voice calls does not require a smartphone, a data plan, or digital literacy. It just requires a phone, which most women already have.

4. ## HEALTH WORKERS ARE OVERSTRETCHED

The community health workers and midwives managing maternal care on the ground are not failing for lack of effort. They are working within a system that does not give them the tools to keep up.

The WHO recommends at least 23 health workers per 10,000 people to cover essential health services adequately.\[11\] Across sub-Saharan Africa, the average is around 10 per 10,000 which is less than half of what is recommended.\[12\] In rural districts in Ghana, community health nurses often manage caseloads of 200 to 400 patients or more, with paper records and no digital system to help them prioritise.

A study across several African countries found that fewer than 20% of high-risk patients received follow-up contact within the recommended timeframe.\[13\] This is not because workers did not care. It is because there was no system telling them who needed attention most, and not enough hours in the day to reach everyone manually.

5. ## PREGNANCY LOSS IS BEING OVERLOOKED

Pregnancy loss is far more common than most people realize, and the care that follows it is far less available than it should be.

Stillbirth affects about 1 in every 72 pregnancies worldwide, with rates in sub-Saharan Africa running well above the global average.\[14\]

The health risks do not end when a pregnancy is lost. Up to 50% of women who experience a miscarriage or stillbirth develop significant anxiety or depression in the months that follow.\[15\] Physical complications, including infection and heavy bleeding, can still occur and still require monitoring. Yet structured follow-up care after pregnancy loss is available in fewer than 10% of health systems in low-income countries.\[16\] Women going through one of the hardest experiences of their lives are largely left to manage their recovery without support.

2. # **SOLUTION OVERVIEW**

   1. ## THE CORE IDEA

MamaCare AI is a voice-based maternal care platform. It keeps women connected to health support throughout their pregnancy and recovery through regular, automated phone calls, while giving healthcare workers a clear, real-time picture of their patients so they can act quickly when someone needs help.

The platform was built around one central question: what does almost every woman in a low-resource setting already have access to? The answer is a basic phone. Not a smartphone. Not an internet connection. Just a phone that can make and receive calls. MamaCare AI works on that phone, in her language, without asking her to download anything or learn a new system. She just has to pick up when it calls.

2. ## WHAT IT DOES FOR PREGNANT WOMEN

When a woman is enrolled in MamaCare AI, by a health worker, a community volunteer, or through a simple call herself, she begins receiving regular check-in calls. The timing of these calls is tied to her stage of pregnancy, so the questions she is asked are always relevant to where she is in her journey.

The calls feel like a conversation, not a questionnaire. The platform asks how she has been feeling, whether she has noticed any changes, whether she has any concerns. It listens to her answers and responds in a way that makes sense for her situation. If she mentions a headache, it asks a follow-up question. If she describes something that sounds like a warning sign, the system takes note.

When the platform detects something that may need attention, it acts. It may give her clear guidance on what to do, alert her health worker right away, or in urgent situations, contact the nearest facility with her details and a summary of what she reported. This happens automatically, without her needing to know who to call or how to escalate the situation herself.

Between these check-ins, the platform also supports her more broadly. It reminds her when to take her medications, gives her practical guidance on nutrition, and explains clearly which symptoms mean she should go to a hospital without waiting. It is the kind of steady, reliable support that the health system has always meant to provide but has rarely had the capacity to deliver at scale.

3. ## WHAT IT DOES FOR WOMEN AFTER PREGNANCY LOSS

When a pregnancy ends in loss, MamaCare AI does not go silent. The woman is moved into a dedicated care programme that is designed specifically for what she is going through.

The calls continue, but they shift in tone and focus. They check on how her body is recovering, watch for physical warning signs like heavy bleeding or fever, and gently assess how she is doing emotionally. They offer support that is compassionate and free of pressure, giving her space to talk if she wants to, while making sure that someone is still paying attention to her health.

The platform also allows her to register a close partner or relative who receives tips on how to help the mother cope with the loss of pregnancy. The inclusion of testimonials from mothers who experienced the same situation also helps the mother to cope.

When she is ready, the platform also helps her think about the future, including family planning, understanding her fertility, and preparing for another pregnancy if that is something she wants. The goal is to make sure that no woman has to navigate the aftermath of pregnancy loss on her own.

4. ## WHAT IT DOES FOR HEALTHCARE WORKERS

Healthcare workers access MamaCare AI through a simple web-based dashboard that works on any device, whether a desktop computer at the clinic, a tablet in the field, or a basic smartphone.

The dashboard shows them all their patients, organised by how urgently each one needs attention. Women who may be at risk appear at the top, with a clear note explaining what the platform has detected. Workers do not have to sift through a long list to find out who needs a call. The system tells them.

When the platform identifies a situation that needs immediate attention, the health worker receives an alert right away. They can see the full picture of what the patient reported, the history of previous calls, and what the system flagged. They have everything they need to respond quickly and with confidence.

Each patient's profile keeps a full record of every interaction, every symptom reported, and every action taken. Workers can add their own notes, log follow-up conversations, and track whether referrals were completed. Nothing gets lost. No one slips through the cracks.

For those managing a team or a facility, the platform also provides summary reports on how patients across the caseload are doing, where the gaps in follow-up are, and how outcomes are trending over time. These reports can be exported for district or national reporting, removing the need for manual data collection and compilation.

5. ## HOW THE SYSTEM HANDLES LANGUAGE

MamaCare AI is designed to communicate in the languages that women actually speak. The platform supports local languages, and voice content is developed with input from local communities to make sure it sounds natural and appropriate, not translated or foreign. A woman does not need to speak English or be able to read to use it. She just needs to be able to have a conversation, which is something everyone can do.

3. #  **SECTION E: SCALABILITY AND SUSTAINABILITY**

   1. ##  REACHING MORE PEOPLE WITHOUT LOSING QUALITY

One of the most important things about MamaCare AI is that it is designed to grow without becoming harder to manage or more expensive to run.

The most significant reason for this is the platform's foundation. Because it works through voice calls on basic mobile phones, it does not require any new infrastructure to expand into a new community. The moment MamaCare AI is active in a new area, it can reach every woman in that area who has a phone. No new towers, no new devices, no new setup required.

Adding a new language to the platform means recording voice content in that language and adapting the conversation flow for cultural context. Adding a new region means enrolling health workers in the dashboard and beginning to enroll patients. Neither of these requires rebuilding the platform from scratch. The core system stays the same and the new components are added on top of it.

For healthcare workers, growth does not mean more work. It means less. Because the platform handles the routine check-ins that would otherwise require manual follow-up, a health worker using MamaCare AI can stay meaningfully connected to a much larger group of patients than she could manage on her own. The system monitors continuously and only surfaces the cases that genuinely need her attention. As the platform reaches more patients, this benefit grows with it.

2. ## STAYING FINANCIALLY VIABLE

Many health programmes start with strong funding and good intentions, then quietly wind down when the money runs out. MamaCare AI is designed to avoid that pattern by building toward financial sustainability from the start.

The primary revenue model is through health system contracts. National and district health authorities pay a fee to deploy the platform as part of their maternal health programmes. This is a straightforward model that fits within existing public health procurement processes and provides steady, recurring income. As governments and health insurers increasingly recognise digital health services as reimbursable, MamaCare AI is positioned to be included in those frameworks, reducing the burden on public budgets and making the service sustainable at the patient level without requiring out-of-pocket payment.

During the early stages of growth, development partners and health-focused funders provide an additional layer of financial support.

3. ## BECOMING PART OF THE HEALTH SYSTEM

Financial sustainability matters, but it is not enough on its own. A platform that sits on the edge of the health system, used occasionally and optional to ignore, will not last. MamaCare AI is designed to become genuinely embedded in how health workers do their jobs.

When a health worker relies on the dashboard every day to manage her caseload, when the alerts from the platform are part of how she decides who to call, when patient records in the system are the first thing she checks before a clinic visit, the platform stops being a tool and starts being infrastructure. At that point, removing it would mean a real loss of capacity. That is a very different position from being something that can be quietly defunded without anyone noticing.

Training for health workers is designed to be delivered by local trainers, so it does not depend permanently on external support. Documentation and guidance can be available in local languages.

4. ## BUILDING TRUST WITH WOMEN

A platform that women do not trust will not work, no matter how well it is built. Trust is earned through consistency, through cultural sensitivity, and through content that feels genuinely helpful rather than impersonal or intrusive.

Voice content for MamaCare AI is developed with input from local communities, from women with lived experience of pregnancy and pregnancy loss, and from clinical advisors who understand the specific health context. The calls are designed to feel like a caring check-in, not a data collection exercise. Women who feel that the platform is looking out for them are more likely to answer the calls, engage honestly, and act on the guidance they receive.

As the platform grows and more outcome data becomes available, it will be possible to demonstrate clearly and specifically where early detection made a difference, where follow-up prevented a complication, and where a woman got help she would not otherwise have received. That evidence strengthens the case for continued investment and builds the kind of credibility that makes governments and health systems want to expand the programme rather than wind it down.

The goal is not simply to run a successful pilot. The goal is to build something that health systems, health workers, and the women it serves genuinely cannot imagine doing without.

 

4. # **SECTION F: ETHICAL AND RESPONSIBLE AI CONSIDERATIONS**

This section outlines how MamaCare AI approaches that obligation across four areas: data privacy and security, bias and fairness, transparency and explainability, and alignment with legal and regulatory requirements across sub-Saharan Africa.

1. ## DATA PRIVACY AND SECURITY

Women in sub-Saharan Africa have historically had limited say over how their health information is collected, stored, and shared. In many communities, a woman's pregnancy status, her experience of miscarriage or stillbirth, or her mental health struggles carry significant social weight. If that information reaches the wrong people, whether a family member, an employer, or a community figure, it can cause real harm. The platform takes this seriously at every level of its design.

2. ## What Data Is Collected and Why

MamaCare AI collects only the information it needs to deliver care. This includes basic enrollment details such as name, phone number, location, and pregnancy stage, as well as symptom information reported during calls and interaction records that allow health workers to track a patient's history. No information is collected beyond what is directly relevant to monitoring and supporting the woman's health.

3. ## How Data Is Protected

All data collected by the platform is encrypted, both while it is being transmitted and while it is stored. Access to patient information is limited strictly to the health workers and administrators assigned to that patient's care. Workers are required to use secure login credentials, and access permissions are set according to each person's role. A community health worker can see the patients on her caseload. She cannot access records belonging to patients assigned to a different worker or facility.

Call recordings are stored securely and are not used for any purpose other than improving the accuracy of the platform's voice understanding. Women are informed at enrollment that their calls may be recorded and what those recordings are used for.

4. ## Consent

No woman is enrolled in MamaCare AI without her knowledge and agreement. At enrollment, she is told in plain language what the platform does, what information it will collect, how that information will be used, and who will have access to it. She is told that participation is voluntary and that she can withdraw at any time without affecting the care she receives through other health services. Consent is obtained verbally, in her language, so that literacy is not a barrier to understanding what she is agreeing to.

5. ## Data Retention and Deletion

Patient data is kept for as long as it is clinically relevant and in line with the data retention standards of the country in which the platform is operating. Women or their health workers can request that records be reviewed or deleted, and those requests are handled promptly. Data is never sold, shared with third parties for commercial purposes, or used in ways that were not disclosed at enrollment.

5. # **BIAS AND FAIRNESS**

   1. ## How MamaCare AI Addresses Bias

The risk level prediction models used by the platform are developed and tested using data that reflects the diversity of the populations it serves. This means including data from rural and urban settings, from women across different age groups and ethnic backgrounds, and from contexts with varying levels of existing health infrastructure. Where gaps in available data exist, they are acknowledged honestly rather than papered over.

The platform is designed to treat every woman as an individual. Its risk assessments are based on her specific reported symptoms and history, not on assumptions drawn from group characteristics. A woman is not assessed as higher or lower risk because of where she lives, her age, or her ethnicity. The system assesses what she has told it about how she is feeling.

Health workers are also trained to understand that the platform's assessments are a support tool, not a final verdict. Clinical judgment remains with the human being, and workers are encouraged to question or override the system's recommendations when their own assessment of a patient suggests a different course of action.

2. ## Language and Cultural Fairness

Bias in AI is not only statistical. It can also be linguistic and cultural. A system that works well in English but poorly in Twi, Hausa, Swahili, or Amharic is not a fair system. MamaCare AI invests in developing voice content and language understanding across the local languages of each deployment context, with input from native speakers and local health professionals. The goal is a platform that works as well for a woman speaking Dinka in South Sudan as it does for one speaking Twi in Ghana.

6. # **TRANSPARENCY AND EXPLAINABILITY**

   1. ## Why Explainability Matters

When an AI system tells a health worker that a patient is at high risk, the health worker needs to be able to understand why. A recommendation that cannot be explained cannot be properly evaluated. A health worker who does not understand the basis for an alert may either follow it blindly, which removes her clinical judgment from the process, or dismiss it out of habit, which defeats the purpose of the system entirely.

Transparency also matters for the women being served. A woman has a right to understand, in terms she can follow, what the platform has noticed about her health, why it is making a particular recommendation, and what will happen next.

When MamaCare AI flags a patient as high risk, the dashboard does not simply display a risk score. It shows the health worker the specific symptoms or patterns that led to that assessment, written in plain language. For example, rather than displaying a numerical score, the dashboard might note that a patient has reported persistent headaches and swelling in her hands over the past two weeks, which are symptoms associated with preeclampsia and which have prompted this alert. The worker can see exactly what the system is responding to and make her own judgment about what to do with that information.

Women receive the same principle of plain explanation. When the platform gives guidance during a call, it explains the reason behind it. It does not simply tell a woman to go to the hospital. It tells her what symptom she described, why that symptom is worth taking seriously, and what she should do and how quickly.

2. ## Limitations

MamaCare AI is open about what it cannot do. The platform does not claim to diagnose conditions. It identifies patterns that may be cause for concern and brings them to the attention of a qualified health worker. This distinction is made clear to health workers during training and to women during the enrollment process. The platform is a monitoring and support tool. Clinical decisions are made by people.

Where the system is uncertain, it says so. An alert that is based on limited information is presented differently from one that is based on a clear pattern across multiple interactions. Health workers are given enough context to calibrate how seriously to take each alert rather than treating all of them as equally urgent.

7. # **ALIGNMENT WITH LEGAL AND REGULATORY REQUIREMENTS**

   1. ## The Regulatory Landscape in Sub-Saharan Africa

Data protection and health information regulation across sub-Saharan Africa has developed significantly in recent years. A growing number of countries have introduced national data protection legislation that governs how personal information, including health data, can be collected, stored, processed, and shared. MamaCare AI is designed to operate in compliance with these frameworks in each country where it can be deployed.

Key legislation relevant to the platform's operations includes the following;

Ghana enacted the Data Protection Act (Act 843\) in 2012, which requires organisations that collect personal data to register with the Data Protection Commission, obtain informed consent from individuals, and maintain appropriate security measures. Health data is treated as sensitive data under this Act and is subject to stricter requirements (Data Protection Commission Ghana, 2012).\[17\]

Nigeria passed the Nigeria Data Protection Regulation (NDPR) in 2019, later strengthened by the Nigeria Data Protection Act in 2023, which establishes detailed requirements for the lawful processing of personal data, mandatory data breach notification, and the rights of individuals to access, correct, and delete their data.\[18\]

Kenya introduced the Data Protection Act in 2019, which mirrors many principles of international data protection standards and requires data processors handling health information\[19\] 

Rwanda's Law No. 058/2021 on the Protection of Personal Data and Privacy similarly establishes clear rights for individuals and obligations for organisations that process personal data, with specific attention to sensitive categories of information including health data.\[20\]

2. ## How MamaCare AI Meets These Requirements

Across all of these frameworks, several common principles apply: informed consent before data collection, a clear and limited purpose for collecting data, appropriate security measures to protect data, the right of individuals to access and correct their information, and restrictions on sharing data with third parties without consent. MamaCare AI's data practices are built around these principles.

Before deploying in any new country, the platform must undergo a legal review to ensure that its data collection, storage, and processing practices align with local requirements. Where local law requires registration with a data protection authority, that registration will be completed before the platform begins collecting data. Where local law requires specific disclosures to be made to users, those disclosures are incorporated into the enrollment process.

3. ## Alignment with Regional Health Frameworks

Beyond data protection law, MamaCare AI is also designed to align with regional health frameworks that govern maternal and reproductive health services in sub-Saharan Africa. The African Union's Agenda 2063\[21\] and the Maputo Plan of Action on Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights\[22\] both establish commitments to improving maternal health outcomes across the continent. The platform's focus on early detection, continuous monitoring, and equitable access to care directly supports these commitments.

The platform is also designed to complement rather than replace existing community health worker programmes and national maternal health strategies. In Ghana, this means working within the framework established by the Ghana Community Health Planning and Services programme.\[23\] In other deployment contexts, the same principle applies: MamaCare AI is a tool that strengthens existing systems, not one that operates independently of them.

4. ## Ethical Oversight

MamaCare AI is subject to ethical review before deployment in any new context. This review should be conducted by an independent ethics committee that includes clinical professionals, community representatives, legal advisors, and individuals with expertise in AI ethics. The review assesses whether the platform's practices are consistent with the rights and interests of the women it serves, whether consent processes are genuinely informed and voluntary, and whether the platform's use of AI is appropriate and properly overseen.

Ethical review is not a one-time exercise. As the platform evolves and as deployment contexts change, reviews must be repeated to ensure that the platform continues to meet the standards expected of a system handling sensitive health information at scale.
